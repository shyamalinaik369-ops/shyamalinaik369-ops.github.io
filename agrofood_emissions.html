01 · The Question

Agriculture feeds the world.
It also heats it.

The food system — everything from growing crops and raising livestock, to packaging, transporting, and disposing of food — is responsible for roughly 62% of the world's annual CO₂ emissions. Yet when people think about climate change, they often focus on cars and power plants. The agricultural sector rarely gets the same attention.

This project asked a deceptively simple question: Where exactly are food-related emissions coming from, how have they changed over 30 years, and what drives them? Using a dataset combining FAO and IPCC data across 7,000+ records and 31 variables, the goal was to turn raw numbers into clear, actionable insight — for policymakers, researchers, and anyone trying to understand humanity's relationship with the climate.

What makes this different

Rather than simply loading a spreadsheet into Python, this project began by designing a proper relational database — the kind used in real-world data systems — before performing any analysis. This data engineering step is what separates a data analyst from a basic spreadsheet user.

02 · Database Design

Before any analysis:
building the architecture.

Most students import a CSV and start plotting immediately. This project took a different approach — first designing a normalised relational database using SQLite, splitting the data into three logically separate tables that mirror how production databases work in industry. Each table has a clear purpose, and they are linked through shared keys — just like the databases behind real business applications.

Why This Matters

This three-table normalised design eliminates data redundancy, maintains referential integrity via foreign keys, and mirrors the structure of real production databases. It shows the ability to think about data architecture — not just data analysis.

03 · Key Findings

10 findings that tell
a coherent story.

The EDA covered temporal trends, correlation analysis, regional comparisons, bivariate and multivariate analysis, and spatial mapping. Here are the most significant discoveries — told in plain language.

Top 10 CO₂ Emitting Countries — Total Agri-food Emissions 1990–2020 (kilotons)
Global Trend
01
China and Brazil dominate — for very different reasons

China's emissions are driven by industrial food production, energy use, and its enormous rural population. Brazil's are driven primarily by deforestation and savanna burning — land cleared to grow soy and raise cattle. Two countries, similar emission volumes, completely different root causes.

Correlation
02
As the world urbanises, emissions climb with it

Urban population shows a strikingly strong correlation (0.91) with total emissions — stronger even than rural population (0.71). This tells a nuanced story: urbanisation drives demand for processed, packaged, and transported food — precisely the high-emission categories in this dataset.

Temporal
03
Global temperature and emissions rise in lockstep

The global warming trends chart showed both average temperature and total CO₂ emissions increasing consistently from 1990 to 2020. The trajectory accelerated notably after 2000. If unchanged, the data projects continued warming — directly supporting the scientific consensus behind the Paris Agreement targets.

Fire & Land
04
Fire emissions are volatile; forest loss is steady and dangerous

Savanna and forest fires spike dramatically in certain years (the late 1990s Indonesian fires, for example), creating noisy emission signals. Forestland emissions, by contrast, show a slow but relentless decline — forests being converted to farmland, permanently removing a key carbon sink from the planet.

Regional
05
Asia carries the heaviest burden — but Africa is accelerating

Asia consistently tops both emissions and population figures. But the more concerning trend is in Africa, where both population growth and emissions are accelerating rapidly — with limited access to the clean technologies needed to decouple growth from carbon output.

Emissions Sources
06
Food processing and transport, not farming, are the hidden culprits

The attribute correlation heatmap showed that food_transport, food_household_consumption, pesticides_manufacturing, and food_retail are the variables most strongly correlated with total_emission. The act of farming itself is less the problem than what happens to food after it leaves the field.

Analyst Note — The Surprising Result

The weak direct correlation (0.02) between average temperature rise and total emission seems counterintuitive. This is a critical analytical insight: temperature rise is a global phenomenon driven by cumulative, atmospheric CO₂ — not by individual country emissions in a given year. A country's local temperature rise doesn't neatly correlate with its own emission levels. This finding shows the difference between correlation and causation, and why domain knowledge matters in data analysis.

05 · Country Deep-Dive

Why did Russia's emissions
collapse — then recover?

The Russia case study (1990–2020) is the most analytically interesting section of this project. It demonstrates how a single country's emissions can tell the story of an entire geopolitical era — and why data analysts must understand history, not just numbers.

Russian Federation: 30 Years of Agri-food Emissions — A Story in Three Acts

Russia's emission trajectory from 1990 to 2020 is unlike any other country in this dataset. Rather than a smooth upward trend, it shows a dramatic collapse followed by gradual recovery — a direct mirror of the Soviet Union's dissolution and Russia's turbulent transition to a market economy.

1990–1998
The Soviet Collapse

The dissolution of the USSR triggered an economic freefall. State-managed collective farms collapsed overnight. Agricultural output fell by over 40%. With less farming, less food processing, and less transportation — emissions dropped dramatically. The environmental "benefit" of economic catastrophe.

1998–2008
Slow Recovery

Under Putin's early presidency, economic stabilisation and rising oil revenues allowed gradual agricultural reinvestment. Emissions begin to climb again as food production recovered — but never back to Soviet-era levels, as the agricultural sector was restructured around market efficiency rather than output volume.

2008–2020
Modern Plateau

Emissions stabilise at a new level — lower than the Soviet peak, but higher than the post-collapse trough. Russia becomes a major grain exporter, driving a new wave of agricultural activity. The data shows a country whose environmental footprint is permanently shaped by its political history.

💡
The Russia analysis proves a crucial point: data alone doesn't tell stories — context does. Without knowing the history of Soviet collapse, the emission dip looks like an anomaly. With that context, it becomes one of the most vivid examples of how political events reshape environmental data. This is the difference between data reporting and data analysis.
06 · Skills Demonstrated

What this project
actually proves.

Beyond the environmental insights, this project demonstrates a complete end-to-end data workflow — from engineering the data structure to delivering visual and narrative analysis.

🗃️ Data Engineering

Designed and implemented a 3-table normalised SQLite relational database using Python's sqlite3 library — including primary keys, foreign key relationships, and appropriate data types. This goes significantly beyond what most analytics courses require.

🧹 Data Preprocessing

Applied IQR-based outlier detection, verified data types, standardised formats, removed duplicates, and made a considered decision to retain meaningful environmental outliers (wildfires, deforestation events) rather than blindly removing them.

📊 Exploratory Data Analysis

Conducted 10+ distinct analyses including distribution analysis, correlation heatmaps, category comparisons, temporal trends, bivariate scatter plots, multivariate 3D scatter plots, and geographic spatial mapping — the full toolkit of EDA methods.

🗺️ Spatial Visualisation

Produced a choropleth world map of CO₂ emissions by country in 2020, demonstrating the ability to move beyond standard 2D charts into geographic data representation — a valuable skill in environmental, policy, and business analytics.
