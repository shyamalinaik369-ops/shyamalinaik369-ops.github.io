01 · The Problem

Can macroeconomic data predict
how much Americans spend?

Personal consumption expenditure drives roughly 70% of U.S. GDP. Forecasting it accurately — even one or two quarters ahead — has profound implications for monetary policy, business planning, and economic research. But consumption doesn't move in isolation: it responds to wages, production levels, job market shocks, and savings behaviour, all of which interact in complex, time-varying ways.

This project used the uschange dataset from R's fpp2 package — 186 quarters of percentage changes in five U.S. economic indicators from 1970 to 2016 — to build dynamic regression models with ARIMA errors that forecast consumption from its macroeconomic context.

But the deeper question this project ended up answering was: when three models give different answers, which do you trust?

Central Research Question

Which macroeconomic indicators are the most reliable predictors of U.S. consumption change — and how do we choose between models when raw accuracy and statistical validity give conflicting verdicts?

Stationarity — All Variables Confirmed

Both ADF (p < 0.05, rejects unit root) and KPSS (p > 0.05, confirms stationarity) tests were applied to all five variables. ACF plots showed rapid autocorrelation decay. Conclusion: no differencing or Box-Cox transformation was required — all series were ready for dynamic regression modeling as-is. Note: “stationary” does not mean white noise — some residual correlation may still exist, which is exactly what the ARIMA error component addresses.

03 · Three Models

Each one deliberately
more complex than the last.

Rather than selecting predictors by correlation alone, a data-driven approach tested three predictor combinations to see how complexity affected both accuracy and residual behaviour. Every model used dynamic regression with ARIMA errors, fitted via auto.arima(xreg=...) on the 1970–2014 training set.

01
Model 1 · ARIMA(0,0,3)
Income + Production — the parsimonious baseline

The two strongest theoretically-grounded predictors. Income directly funds spending; production activity reflects the economy's output capacity. Auto ARIMA selected a pure MA(3) error structure — no autoregressive or differencing terms needed, reconfirming stationarity. The positive coefficients (0.1751 for Income, 0.1856 for Production) align precisely with economic theory. The intercept (0.5251) reflects the baseline quarterly growth rate.

ẑₜ = 0.010·ε̂ₜ₋₁ + 0.205·ε̂ₜ₋₂ + 0.171·ε̂ₜ₋₃ + εₜ
σ² = 0.269 · log likelihood = −134.28
AIC: 282.56 BIC: 304.91 RMSE: 0.181 Ljung-Box p: 0.86 ✓ MAPE: 17.3% Theil's U: 0.67
02
Model 2 · ARIMA(2,1,1)
Adding Unemployment — theoretically sound, practically redundant

Unemployment has a strong negative correlation with consumption (−0.54) and clear economic logic — job losses suppress spending. But adding it triggered a more complex ARIMA(2,1,1) error structure including differencing (d=1). Performance actually worsened on the test set: RMSE rose from 0.181 to 0.212. Minor autocorrelation appeared at lags 3 and 16. The lesson: Unemployment's effect may already be captured through Production (their inter-correlation is −0.787), making the predictor redundant rather than additive.

AIC: 281.08 BIC: 303.39 RMSE: 0.212 Ljung-Box p: 0.14 ~ MAPE: 18.4% Theil's U: 0.76
03
Model 3 · ARIMA(4,1,1)
All predictors — impressive numbers, broken residuals

Including Savings alongside all other predictors produced RMSE of 0.080 and AIC of 130.6. On the surface, this looks like the clear winner — nearly half the error of Model 1. But the Ljung-Box test returned p = 0.0066, firmly rejecting the white noise hypothesis. Systematic structure remains in the residuals at lag 7. This means prediction intervals are invalid, generalisation to truly new data is unreliable, and the accuracy is at least partly a statistical artefact of Savings' extreme volatility being "memorised" by the model rather than genuinely learned.

AIC: 130.60 ★ RMSE: 0.080 ★ MAPE: 8.0% ★ Ljung-Box p: 0.007 ✗ FAILS DIAGNOSTICS
04 · The Decision

Rejecting the best number.
Choosing the best model.

This is the moment that separates analysts who count from analysts who think. Model 3 had the lowest RMSE, the lowest AIC, the lowest BIC. By every numerical scoreboard, it "won." Most students stop there. But the residual diagnostics told a different story — and listening to them is what this project is actually about.

Model 1 · ARIMA(0,0,3)
Textbook residual behaviour

ACF shows all lags within the 95% confidence bounds. Histogram approximates normality. Ljung-Box Q* = 1.909 across 5 degrees of freedom — overwhelmingly consistent with white noise. The model has extracted all exploitable signal from the data.

✓ Ljung-Box p = 0.86
Model 2 · ARIMA(2,1,1)
Marginal — mostly acceptable

Small autocorrelation spikes at lags 3 and 16, but the overall Ljung-Box test passes at p = 0.14. Adding Unemployment introduced ARIMA complexity without improving accuracy — a classic case of unnecessary complexity that adds noise without adding signal.

~ Ljung-Box p = 0.14
Model 3 · ARIMA(4,1,1)
Fails the fundamental test

ACF reveals systematic residual structure at lag 7. Ljung-Box Q* = 12.23, p = 0.0066 — the model has NOT absorbed all information in the data. Its apparent accuracy is misleading. Prediction intervals constructed from this model are statistically invalid.

✗ Ljung-Box p = 0.007
Model 1 (Income + Production) is the final model.

Despite Model 3's superior raw numbers, its failure to produce white-noise residuals disqualifies it as a reliable forecasting tool. Model 1 passes every diagnostic test, aligns with economic theory, and produces confidence intervals that correctly contain the actual test observations — a statistically calibrated forecast, even if not visually perfect. Confidence intervals from Model 1 capture the actual test data within both the 80% and 95% bounds — a key validation that the model is not just accurate, but honest about its uncertainty.

05 · Forecasting Results

Seven quarters ahead.
Statistically calibrated.

Model 1 was used to forecast consumption changes for 2015 Q1 through 2016 Q3 — a seven-quarter out-of-sample horizon using actual observed Income and Production values for those quarters as regressors. The forecast tracks the general trend; actual observations fall within the 80% and 95% prediction intervals, confirming statistical calibration.

* Model 3 (rose) visually appears closest to actuals — but its Ljung-Box failure means those predictions cannot be trusted for genuinely unseen future data. Model 1 (gold) delivers reliable, interpretable forecasts backed by sound residual diagnostics.

Reading the Forecasts

Model 1's point forecasts cluster around 0.65–0.75% quarterly change, reflecting the historical baseline. The sharp spike in Q2 2016 (~1.1%) was not captured in the point estimate — but it fell within the 95% prediction interval, meaning the model was not wrong, only appropriately uncertain. This distinction between calibration and precision is a key concept in professional forecasting practice.

06 · The Rigour

Every decision tested.
Nothing assumed.

The project followed a disciplined, multi-criteria model evaluation workflow — the same framework professional econometricians use and most introductory courses never teach.

ACF — Model 1 Residuals · All 24 Lags Within 95% Bounds = White Noise Confirmed

* Blue dashed lines = ±1.96/√n confidence bounds. All bars fall within bounds, confirming no exploitable autocorrelation in Model 1's residuals. Contrast with Model 3, where lag 7 breaches the upper bound — triggering the Ljung-Box failure.

🔬 Dual Stationarity Testing

Both ADF (p < 0.05) and KPSS (p > 0.05) tests were applied to all five variables. Dual confirmation was essential — relying on one test alone is a common and dangerous shortcut. All variables confirmed stationary: no differencing or transformation required before modeling.

📐 Ljung-Box as the Decision Gate

Applied to residuals of all three models. Model 1: Q* = 1.909, p = 0.86. Model 2: Q* = 8.38, p = 0.14. Model 3: Q* = 12.23, p = 0.007. The Ljung-Box test became the decisive factor — overriding all numerical accuracy metrics for Model 3 on principled statistical grounds.

📊 Theil's U Benchmarking

Beyond RMSE, Theil's U was calculated to benchmark each model against a naïve no-change forecast. Model 1 achieved U = 0.67, meaningfully beating the naïve baseline. Model 3's U = 0.29 looks better but is untrustworthy given its diagnostic failure — a valuable illustration of why metrics must be evaluated in context.

📉 AIC / BIC as Supporting Evidence

Information criteria were computed but treated as supporting rather than decisive evidence. Model 3's dramatically lower AIC (130.6 vs 282.56) initially seemed compelling — but AIC alone cannot detect the structural residual problem that Ljung-Box revealed. This is a key lesson: no single criterion is sufficient for model selection.

07 · Skills Demonstrated

What this project
actually proves.

Beyond the forecasting output, this project demonstrates a complete, professional-grade time series workflow — and a model selection decision that reflects genuine statistical maturity rather than mechanical metric optimisation.

📈 Time Series EDA in R

Applied autoplot, ACF/PACF plots, histograms with density overlays, boxplots, scatter plots with regression lines, and correlation matrices across all five variables. Identified Savings as structurally problematic through layered visual and statistical evidence — not just intuition.

⚙️ Dynamic Regression + ARIMA Errors

Built three multivariate time series models using auto.arima() with external regressors (xreg). Understood the architectural distinction between ARIMA's temporal autocorrelation component and the regression component's cross-variable dynamics — not just running the function, but knowing why each part exists.

🧪 Principled Model Rejection

Applied Ljung-Box testing, visual residual inspection, and ACF of residuals to all three models. Made the principled decision to reject Model 3 despite its numerical superiority — demonstrating that statistical validity takes precedence over accuracy metrics alone. This is the judgement that separates a statistician from a number-reporter.

🎯 Calibrated Forecasting

Generated 7-quarter forecasts with 80% and 95% prediction intervals. Verified that actual test observations fell within calibrated intervals. Distinguished between visual precision (how close the line is) and statistical calibration (whether the uncertainty bands are honest) — a nuance most practitioners overlook entirely.
