01 · Architecture

The hourglass that forces
every pixel to earn its place.

The autoencoder is a symmetric feedforward network — an encoder that compresses, and a decoder that reconstructs. The symmetry is intentional: the decoder must mirror the encoder's journey in reverse. The bottleneck layer is the constraint that gives the whole system meaning. If the decoder can reconstruct from only 32 numbers, those 32 numbers must encode something real.

Why Dense, not Convolutional?

A Dense Autoencoder flattens the image to a 1D vector and uses fully connected layers — simpler, but sufficient for MNIST's uniform grayscale digits. The deliberate choice to avoid CNNs keeps the analysis cleaner: every latent neuron has an equal relationship to every pixel, making activation patterns easier to interpret. Convolutional Autoencoders would be the natural next step for more complex image data, as discussed in the future directions section.

Hyperparameter Rationale

Hidden Layers
128 neurons — the right middle ground

Tested configurations of 64, 128, and 256. The 128-neuron hidden layer follows the heuristic of choosing a size between input and output dimensions, and empirically produced the best reconstruction/efficiency balance. 256 added parameters without improving quality; 64 compressed too aggressively before the bottleneck.

Bottleneck
32 neurons — enough to dream, not enough to cheat

Evaluated bottleneck sizes of 16, 20, and 32 neurons. 32 was selected for optimal reconstruction quality and latent space interpretability. At 16 neurons, finer digit details were lost. The final selection of 32 balances compression strength with the ability to produce meaningful, analysable latent vectors — a 24.5× compression from 784 input dimensions.

02 · Training

72% loss reduction.
No overfitting. Clean convergence.

The autoencoder was trained for 20 epochs on 60,000 MNIST images, validated against the 10,000-image test set. Both training and validation loss declined consistently — and crucially, the gap between them narrowed over time rather than widening. This is the hallmark of a model that generalises, not one that memorises.

Training & Validation Loss — Binary Cross-Entropy over 20 Epochs

Training loss: 0.3324 → 0.0909 (−72.7%) · Validation loss: 0.1596 → 0.0897 (−43.8%) · Gap narrows from 0.127 to 0.001 — convergence confirmed.

No Overfitting
Training and validation track together

The validation loss never diverged upward from the training loss — in fact, in early epochs the validation loss was lower than training loss, which is normal in dropout-free models with batch-level stochasticity. By epoch 20, both converge to ~0.090, confirming the model learned genuine features of the data distribution, not training-set quirks.

Reconstruction Quality
Most digits reconstruct faithfully — complexity costs detail

Visual comparison showed that digits 0, 1, 4, 6 reconstructed with high fidelity. Digits 5 and 9 appeared slightly blurrier — a direct consequence of their higher intra-class variability. A single '5' can be written many ways; the autoencoder learns an average representation, and outlier styles get blurred toward the mean. This is the expected compression–detail trade-off, not a failure.

vs PCA

PCA was considered as an alternative dimensionality reduction baseline. For MNIST, PCA can capture ~90% of variance in ~150 components — but at 32 components, the linear projection is noticeably inferior to the autoencoder's non-linear mapping. The autoencoder's ability to learn curved, non-linear manifolds in the data is precisely what enables it to compress to 32 dimensions while preserving enough structure to reconstruct readable digits. PCA cannot do this at the same compression depth.

03 · The Latent Space

The model learned to separate
digits it was never told to separate.

This is the most intellectually striking result. The autoencoder was trained entirely unsupervised — it never saw a label. It only knew: "compress this image, then reconstruct it." Yet when we project the 32-dimensional latent vectors into 2D with t-SNE, the ten digit classes form distinct, largely separated clusters. The model discovered digit identity as a side effect of learning to reconstruct efficiently.

Why do 3, 5, and 8 overlap?

These three digits share fundamental visual sub-features: curved upper segments, open lower loops, and rounded stroke endings. The autoencoder's latent space encodes visual similarity, not digit identity. Three and eight share the curved-top feature; five and three share middle-horizontal strokes; eight contains both loops that five and zero each have separately. The overlap is not a model failure — it's evidence the model learned genuine visual structure.

04 · Neuron Stories

The neurons don't encode digits.
They encode features of digits.

This is the central insight of the latent space analysis, and it's what separates this project from a standard autoencoder tutorial. Examining which neurons activate strongly for which digits reveals a crucial truth: the network learns distributed, reusable visual components — loops, curves, vertical strokes — not class-specific templates.

Bottleneck Activations — Digit "8" (Representative Sample)

* Each bar represents one of the 32 latent neurons' activation for a single image of digit 8. The sparse, uneven pattern — with a few neurons highly active and many near-zero — reflects the distributed encoding strategy. Note: exact active neurons vary per training run.

Neuron 8 — Case Study
Top activating images: mostly 8, some 9

When we ranked all 10,000 test images by activation of Neuron 8, the top results were almost entirely images of digit 8 — with activation values between 21.4 and 23.8. But some digit 9 images also appeared with activation ~21.4. This is the key observation: the neuron isn't a "digit 8 detector". It's responding to the curved loop structure present in both 8 and 9. The neuron learned a sub-feature — a visual component — that happens to be most prominent in 8.

Stochastic Variability
The neurons changed roles on re-execution

When the code was re-run with identical architecture and data, the specific neuron indices that were most active changed completely. Neuron 8 in run 1 was not Neuron 8 in run 2 in terms of what it encoded. This was noticed, understood, and documented — not hidden. The cause: random weight initialisation + Adam's stochastic gradient estimates + non-convex loss landscape = different local minima, same reconstruction quality. The team metaphor: "same tasks, different team members."

05 · Latent Space Experiments

Three experiments that prove
the space is real.

The true test of whether a latent space is meaningful is not reconstruction quality — it's whether you can navigate it. These three experiments probe the latent space from different angles: tweaking, travelling, and shocking individual neurons.

🎛️
Single Neuron Manipulation

Digit 7's latent vector was extracted. Neuron 5 was varied from −3.0 to +3.0 while all other neurons were held fixed. The decoder reconstructed an image at each value.

Result: the top stroke of the 7 gradually curved as the value increased, and straightened as it decreased. The digit never stopped being a 7 — the neuron controls style, not identity.

Neuron 5 → stroke curvature
🌉
Digit Interpolation

The latent vectors of a "2" and a "9" were encoded. Ten intermediate vectors were generated via linear interpolation (α = 0.0 to 1.0) and decoded back to image space.

Result: smooth morphing. The upper loop of the 2 gradually elongated, the lower tail shortened, and the circular base of the 9 emerged. No abrupt jumps — the latent space is continuous.

Digit 2 → Digit 9 · smooth transition
⚡
Perturbation Sensitivity

Each of the 32 neurons was individually perturbed by +1.0 for a fixed digit 7 image. The resulting 32 reconstructions were compared to the unperturbed baseline.

Result: uneven impact. Neurons N8, N15, N24 caused noticeable blurring or shape change. N1, N9, N12 had near-zero effect. Encoded information is not distributed equally.

N8, N15, N24 → high sensitivity

06 · Key Insights

What the latent space
taught us about representation.

🏝️ Unsupervised Class Separation

Without ever seeing a label, the autoencoder arranged the 10 digit classes into separable clusters in latent space. Digits 0, 1, and 6 — visually distinct, low intra-class variance — formed the tightest clusters. This emergence of class structure is a consequence of reconstruction efficiency: separating classes is the most efficient way to compress them.

🧩 Distributed Encoding

No single neuron "represents" a digit. Information is spread across the 32-dimensional vector in overlapping, distributed patterns. This is a fundamental property of deep network representations — and it's exactly what makes the latent space both powerful (robust to individual neuron failure) and hard to interpret (no clean one-to-one mapping).

🎭 Style vs Identity

The neuron manipulation experiment demonstrated that some latent dimensions control stylistic attributes (stroke curvature, thickness, slant) while others control more structural features. A digit can change style without changing identity — the model has implicitly factored these apart, even without being asked to.

🗺️ Continuous Manifold

The interpolation experiment confirmed the latent space is a continuous manifold — you can walk from any point to any other point and pass through plausible intermediate digits. This continuity is what enables controlled generation and smooth morphing. It also aligns with the t-SNE finding that visually similar digits (2 and 9) occupy adjacent regions of the space.

07 · Skills Demonstrated

What this project
actually proves.

🏗️ Neural Network Architecture Design

Designed a symmetric dense autoencoder from first principles — selecting layer sizes via empirical testing and established heuristics, choosing activation functions with justification (ReLU for sparsity, Sigmoid for [0,1] output range), and selecting BCE loss specifically because MNIST pixels are binary-valued. Architecture was not copied from a template.

🔬 Latent Space Analysis

Went far beyond standard autoencoder tutorials with five distinct Phase 2 analyses: t-SNE projection, per-image neuron activation visualisation, top-activating image retrieval, statistical mean activation heatmap, single-neuron manipulation, digit interpolation, and perturbation sensitivity. This depth of feature interpretation is graduate-level work.

🎲 Stochasticity Awareness

Noticed that neuron roles changed on re-execution. Rather than hiding this, diagnosed the cause (random initialisation, Adam's stochastic dynamics, non-convex loss surface) and documented it thoughtfully. This intellectual honesty — understanding why results vary — demonstrates deeper understanding than most practitioners show.

📐 Theoretical Grounding

The project derives and presents the BCE loss function mathematically, explains the Adam optimiser's parameter update rule, and justifies every architecture and hyperparameter choice with reference to established heuristics and empirical evidence. Mathematics was used as a tool for understanding, not just decoration.

Personal Reflection
This project was shaped by a personal decision. The original intention was to apply neural networks to cancer prediction — motivated by a family member's diagnosis. After careful reflection on the ethical weight of high-stakes medical AI, the limitations of black-box models in clinical settings, and the difficulty of achieving the >95% accuracy clinical applications require, the scope was redirected to MNIST. That decision itself demonstrates something important: knowing when not to build something is as valuable as knowing how to build it. The exploration here has deepened the resolve to contribute to medical AI in the future — through explainable, responsible, research-driven approaches.
