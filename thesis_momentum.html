01 · The Problem

Momentum investing works.
Until it catastrophically doesn't.

"Momentum investing" is the idea of buying stocks that have recently been rising and avoiding those that have been falling. It sounds simple — and it actually works. Decades of global research confirm that it consistently generates returns above what the market provides on its own.

But there is a catch. Because momentum strategies concentrate on recent winners, they are dangerously exposed when markets suddenly reverse. During the COVID-19 crash of March 2020, a straightforward momentum strategy on the Indian market lost −26.3% in a single month — and a total of −43.92% from its peak. Years of gains, almost wiped out in weeks.

This thesis asked: Can we keep the gains of momentum while building a shield against its crashes?

The Central Research Question

How do you preserve momentum's return advantage while protecting against its catastrophic, intermittent crashes — specifically within the Indian equity market, where volatility is higher and regime shifts are more abrupt than in developed markets?

02 · The Journey

Five strategies.
Each one smarter than the last.

Rather than jumping straight to a complex model, the research was built layer by layer — each step revealing something new about what works, what doesn't, and why.

01
Baseline
The pure momentum strategy: powerful, but fragile

Starting point: simply buy the top 10 best-performing stocks from India's Nifty 100 index each month, based on their last 12 months of returns. No filters, no rules — just raw momentum. The result? An extraordinary 1,613% total return over 10 years, growing an initial investment more than 16 times. The market benchmark managed only 196% in the same period. But the Achilles' heel was clear — during the COVID-19 crash, the strategy fell by nearly 44% from its peak. Great in calm markets. Devastating in a storm.

CAGR: 32.9% Max Loss: −43.92% Alpha: 21.15% Sharpe: 1.39
02
Sector Filters
Diversify by sector, filter by risk — safer, but at a cost

The next idea: don't just pick the best stocks — first identify the best sectors (like Technology, Energy, Financials), then pick the best stocks within those sectors. This spreads risk more intelligently. An additional "volatility filter" then excluded stocks that moved too erratically compared to the market — measured using a metric called Beta. The maximum loss improved to −37.90%, and the strategy became more defensive (Beta: 0.83). The trade-off: raw returns dropped, as some high-momentum stocks were excluded for being too volatile. This confirmed a key truth: you can't have maximum safety and maximum returns at the same time.

CAGR: 21.1% Max Loss: −37.90% Beta: 0.83 Sharpe: 1.14
03
Machine Learning
Teaching a model to predict tomorrow's winners

This step introduced machine learning — training a model on historical stock data to predict which stocks are most likely to outperform the market next quarter. Three models were compared: Logistic Regression, Random Forest, and XGBoost. Surprisingly, the simplest model won. The Logistic Regression correctly predicted outperformers 58% of the time — a modest edge, but consistent. The more complex models (Random Forest, XGBoost) actually did worse, because they memorised historical patterns too closely and struggled with new data — a classic problem called "overfitting." The key lesson: in noisy, unpredictable markets, simplicity and robustness beat complexity every time.

Best Model: Logistic Regression Accuracy: 58.1% AUC: 0.53 CAGR: 20.1% Max Loss: −36.55%
03 · Machine Learning Deep Dive

Why did the simple model
beat the complex ones?

Financial markets are inherently noisy — prices move for thousands of reasons, many of them unpredictable. When a complex model like XGBoost is trained on historical stock data, it finds incredibly detailed patterns... but many of those patterns are just random noise that won't repeat.

A simpler Logistic Regression, with fewer variables to fit, is forced to focus on the genuinely persistent signals — things like 6-month returns and short-term volatility — rather than the noise. This makes it more reliable on data it's never seen before.

05 · The Final Strategy

The strategy that knows
when to step aside.

All previous strategies had one thing in common: they were always fully invested in stocks. The final breakthrough was introducing a "regime-aware" rule — a simple but powerful mechanism to detect when the market is in danger, and automatically move to safety.

The Rule: 200-Day Moving Average
RISK-ON (Bull Market)
Market index is above its 200-day average → Hold Quality Momentum stocks fully
RISK-OFF (Bear Market)
Market index falls below its 200-day average → Exit all stocks, move to cash-equivalent (LiquidBees ETF)

The 200-day Simple Moving Average is one of the most widely used trend indicators in investing. When the market is above its long-term average, it signals broad confidence and upward momentum. When it falls below, it signals deteriorating conditions — a warning to step back.

This rule is not about predicting the future. It's about reacting intelligently to the present. During the COVID-19 crash of March 2020, this signal triggered a full exit before the worst of the collapse — cutting the maximum loss from −38% to just −17%.

There is a known trade-off: occasionally, markets briefly dip below their average and immediately recover — called a "whipsaw" — causing the strategy to sell unnecessarily. This is the transparent cost of the protection. The benefit during a real crash far outweighs this friction.
